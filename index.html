I live at intersections. The most interesting problems sit at boundaries that most people treat as walls: where software meets ISA, where architecture meets RTL, where transistor physics meets the abstractions we build on top of it. I like understanding the full path, from the application a user touches, through the compiler, down through the instruction set, into the microarchitecture, through the register-transfer logic, all the way to the electrons moving through doped silicon. Every layer makes promises to the one above it. Performance breaks when those promises don't hold.

I think about biology the same way. A cell is a system, arguably the most sophisticated one ever built. DNA is storage. mRNA is the instruction stream. The ribosome is a protein compiler: it reads codons, resolves amino acid lookups, and folds an output structure that executes a function. The cell even has error correction, caching (chaperone proteins), and garbage collection (lysosomes). Each of us is a deeply concurrent program running on wetware, and I find that both humbling and thrilling.

This is why I find architecture worth working on. The right architecture scales amazingly. Biology proved it: one cellular architecture, refined over 3.8 billion years, runs everything from bacteria to blue whales. The same principle holds in computing. You can optimize a kernel, tune a compiler pass, or shrink a transistor, but the ceiling on performance for most systems will always be architectural. A good architecture compounds across generations. A bad one taxes everything built on top of it, forever.

Right now I'm a grad student at Carnegie Mellon studying electrical and computer engineering, working with Phil Gibbons and Andy Pavlo on computer architecture, compiler optimization, and data-intensive systems. Before CMU, I worked on GPU and SoC architecture teams at NVIDIA and Qualcomm. At NVIDIA I focused on pre-silicon performance for Rubin and Feynman; at Qualcomm I worked on system-level performance, inference efficiency, and architecture trade studies. Across both, I kept coming back to the same question: how to make systems scale across the whole design, not just win locally in one block.

Something I Built

LLM inference on mobile GPUs wastes enormous power. The GPU clocks at full speed even when memory bandwidth, not compute, is the bottleneck. The arithmetic intensity of LLM decode is around 10 ops/byte on hardware capable of 48. That gap is pure waste.

I designed a closed-loop system that detects this mismatch across the GPU-NoC-LLC-DRAM stack and dials back the GPU dynamically, without losing throughput.

US20250356449A1 · Qualcomm Inc · Filed May 2024

A composite metric across memory latency, GPU read bandwidth, and ALU utilization drives clock scaling. A second feedback loop using retired instructions per cycle validates the adjustment. If throughput drops too much, it rolls back.

80% better perf/watt · ~7.5% performance cost
